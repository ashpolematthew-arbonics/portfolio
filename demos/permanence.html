🪲 Permanence & disturbance risk real data

A carbon credit only counts if the carbon stays put. Europe's spruce forests are being killed by the bark beetle (Ips typographus) at unprecedented rates — a direct reversal risk that sizes a project's buffer pool. Below: real Sentinel-2 detection of the Harz outbreak (Germany) using my own EO pipeline, then a Monte-Carlo model that turns disturbance risk into the buffer withholding a project must carry.

How this works — data & method

Detection (real). My bark_beetle_eo.py module computes CIre, NDRE, NBR and NDVI from cloud-masked Sentinel-2 L2A, forms z-scores against a seasonal baseline, and classifies each spruce-canopy pixel into green / yellow / grey-dead attack stages (Bárta et al. 2021, 2022). Run over the Harz (2018 baseline → 2022 detection), it finds ~28% of the spruce canopy bark-beetle-affected, ~8% already grey-dead across ~22,000 ha. The time series shows the affected cohort's chlorophyll index collapsing from 2019 while healthy stands hold steady — the reversal, seen from orbit.

Composite risk. The EO anomaly (mean CIre z-score, % affected) is combined 50/50 with stand-level risk factors — spruce fraction, an age premium (>60 yr stands 1.5× more susceptible), a 3× post-windthrow multiplier for breeding substrate, and additive drought (VPD) stress — following Hlásny et al. (2021) and Seidl et al. (2016).

Buffer sizing (Monte-Carlo). Composite risk sets an annual disturbance hazard; each simulated year a reversal may occur, removing a random 30–90% of standing stock, compounding on what remains. Over 2,000 simulated projects we take the distribution of cumulative reversal across the crediting period. The recommended buffer pool is the 95th-percentile cumulative loss — the withholding needed so that 95% of futures are covered — the quantitative backbone of a non-permanence risk assessment (cf. Verra AFOLU Non-Permanence Risk Tool).
