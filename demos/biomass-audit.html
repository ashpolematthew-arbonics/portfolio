🛰️ Biomass-map disagreement audit real data

How confident are you in the tonnes? Two published, independent aboveground-biomass (AGB) maps — Spawn & Gibbs (2010) and NASA GEDI L4B (2019–2023) — are overlaid on a real dryland-savanna window in northern Ghana (the Bongo–Bolgatanga–Yendi belt of the Great Green Wall). They agree on the regional total to within 2% — yet disagree by roughly 48% pixel-by-pixel. Swipe the maps, then see what that means for crediting.

How this works — data & method

Real, published data, processed in Google Earth Engine and exported to static assets (this page runs entirely in your browser):

  • Spawn & Gibbs (2020) harmonized aboveground biomass, ~2010 — NASA/ORNL/biomass_carbon_density/v1
  • GEDI L4B (v2) gridded aboveground biomass density, 2019–2023 — LARSE/GEDI/GEDI04_B_002
  • ESA WorldCover 2021 for land-cover stratification — ESA/WorldCover/v200

Both AGB products are resampled to a common 1 km grid over the window. I draw ~2,400 valid samples, compute agreement metrics (bias, MAE, RMSE, R²) and stratify them by land cover. The scatter and bars are those real samples; the maps are the real rasters.

Why it matters. The maps agree on the regional mean (≈7.5 Mg/ha, within 2%) but correlate weakly at the pixel scale (R² ≈ 0.29, MAE ≈ 48%). Carbon projects are sited and stratified locally, so which map you trust materially changes a project's baseline stock. Translating AGB → carbon (×0.47) → CO₂e (×44/12), the typical local discrepancy is ≈6 tCO₂e/ha — for a 10,000 ha footprint that's tens of thousands of tonnes of ambiguity before a single plot is measured. This is the argument for field calibration and lidar, and for honest uncertainty deductions.
